> __~definition~.__ Let ${p}$ be a function, where ${\tx{dom}{\px{p}} \subseteq \reals}$ and ${\tx{codom}\px{p} \subseteq \reals.}$ Then ${p}$ is a polynomial function if ${\ell}$ is of the form:
> 
> $$
> 	p(x) = a_{n}x^n + a_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \ldots + a_2x^2 + a_1x + a_0,
> $$
> 
> where each ${a}$ is a real number called a _coefficient_, and ${n}$ is a natural number corresponding to the _degree_ of the polynomial.

## Linear Functions
> __~definition~.__ Let ${\ell}$ be a function, where ${\tx{dom}\px{\ell} \subseteq \reals}$ and ${\tx{codom}\px{\ell} \subseteq \reals.}$  We say that ${\ell}$ is a _linear function_ if ${\ell}$ is of the form:
> 
> $$
> 	\ell{\px{x}} = ax + b,
> $$
> 
> where ${a}$ and ${b}$ are real number constants.

Linear functions are polynomial functions of degree 1. They are also the easiest functions to think about and grasp. So much so that their procedures â€” linear equations â€” have spawned an entire field of study, called [linear algebra](./math/linear_algebra/axioms).

## Horizontal and Vertical Lines
> __~horizontal line~.__ A horizontal line is defined as the linear function ${h(x) = a,}$ where ${a}$ is a constant.

> __~vertical line~.__ A vertical line is defined as the relation ${x = a,}$ where ${a}$ is a constant.

~remark~. The horizontal line ${h}$ is defined as a function, but the vertical line is not. Why? Because a vertical line is _not a function_. It maps a single element of its domain to all of the elements of its codomain, and this does not satisfy the definition of a function.

### Monotonicity of Linear Functions
> __~monotonicity~.__ Let ${\ell\px{x}=ax + b}$ be a linear function, with ${\tx{dom}\px{\ell} \subseteq \reals}$ and ${\tx{codom}\px{\ell} \subseteq \reals.}$
> - If ${a \lt 0,}$ then ${\ell}$ is _decreasing_.
> - If ${a = 0,}$ then ${\ell}$ is _constant_.
> - If ${a \gt 0,}$ then ${\ell}$ is _increasing_.

### ð‘¥-intercept
> __definition.__ Let ${\ell\px{x}=ax ~\pl~ b}$ be a linear function, with ${\tx{dom}\px{\ell} \subseteq \reals}$ and ${\tx{codom}\px{\ell} \subseteq \reals.}$ The ${x}$-intercept of ${\ell}$ is the set ${\set{x \in \tx{dom}{\px{f}} : \ell{\px{x}}=0}.}$

~example~. Let ${f(x)=2x ~\pl~ 1.}$ The ${x}$-intercept is ${\set{\mi\frac{1}{2}},}$ since:

$$
\eqs{
	0 &= 2x ~\pl~ 1 \\
	0 ~\mi~ 1 &= 2x ~\pl~ 1 ~\mi~ 1 \\
	\mi1 &= 2x ~\pl~ 0 \\
	\mi1 &= 2x \\[0.5em]
	\frac{\mi1}{2} &= \frac{2x}{2} \\[0.5em]
	\mi\frac{1}{2} &= x
}
$$

### Derivatives of Linear Functions
> __~derivative: linear function~.__ Let ${\ell\px{x}=ax + b}$ be a linear function, with ${\tx{dom}\px{\ell} \subseteq \reals}$ and ${\tx{codom}\px{\ell} \subseteq \reals.}$ Then ${a}$ corresponds to the derivative of ${\ell.}$ Where ${x_1, x_2 \in \tx{dom}\px{\ell}, x_1 \le x_2,}$ ${a}$ has the property:
> 
> $$
> 	a = \frac{f(x_2) - f(x_1)}{x_2 - x_1}.
> $$

<Algo>

__~linslope~.__ Let ${a=\ix{x_1, y_1}}$ and ${b=\ix{x_2, y_2}}$ be arrays of length 2, where ${a,b \in \df{number} \times \df{number}.}$ The procedure ${\df{linslope}}$ returns the derivative of the line passing through ${a}$ and ${b.}$

1. __function__ ${\df{linslope}(a,b)}$
	1. __if__ ${\len\px{a}=2 \land \len\px{b}=2}$ __then__
		1. ${\let{\Delta y}{a\ix{1}-b\ix{1}}}$
		2. ${\let{\Delta x}{a\ix{0}-b\ix{0}}}$
		3. __return__ ${\dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}}$

</Algo>

### Parallel and Parpendicular Lines
> __~relation~.__ Let ${f(x) = a_fx ~\pl~ b_f}$ and ${g(x) = a_g x ~\pl~ b_g}$ be linear functions. If, and only if, ${a_f = b_f,}$ then ${f}$ and ${g}$ are _parallel_, and we write ${f \parallel g.}$ If, and only if, ${a_f \by a_g = -1,}$ then ${f}$ and ${g}$ are _perpendicular_ and we write ${f \perp g.}$

## Exponent Properties
Working with polynomials of degree ${n}$ requires comfort with exponent manipulation. Below is a quick review of common algebraic techniques associated with exponents. Note the necessary conditions.

<div className={'tile'}>
$$
	x^a \by x^b = x^{a ~\pl~ b}
$$

$$
	\frac{x^b}{x^a} = x^{b ~\mi~ a} \quad \nc x \neq 0

$$
	\px{x^a}^b = \px{x}^{a \by b}
$$

$$
	x^0 = 1 \quad \nc x \neq 0
$$

$$
	x^{-n} = \frac{1}{x^n} \quad \nc x \neq 0
$$

$$
	\px{xy}^n = \px{x^n}\px{y^n}
$$

$$
	\ar{\frac{x}{y}}^n = \frac{x^n}{y^n} \quad \nc y \neq 0
$$

$$
	x^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{x} \quad \nc n \neq 0

### Multiplying Polynomials
We can quickly multiply polynomials with a 2-way table.

~example~. ${(2x + 3)(3x -7).}$

$$
	\begin{array}{c:c:}
		~  & \pl3x   & \mi4 \\ \hdashline
		\pl2x & \pl6x^2 & \mi8x \\ \hdashline
		\pl3 & \pl9x   & \mi12
	\end{array}
$$

Concatenate the cells row by row, top-right to bottom-left:

$$
	\begin{array}{c:c:}
		~  & \pl3x   & \mi4 \\ \hdashline
		\pl2x & \rd{\pl6x^2} & \yd{\mi8x} \\ \hdashline
		\pl3 & \yd{\pl9x}   & \bd{\mi12}
	\end{array}
	= \rd{\pl6x^2}\yd{\mi8x}\yd{\pl9x}\bd{\mi12}
	= 6x^2-x-12
$$

The approach extends to larger polynomials.

~example~.

$$
	(x^3-2x^2+2x+3)(2x^2-3x-4) \\[1em]
	\parallel
	\\[1em]
	\begin{array}{c:c:c:}
		~        & \pl 2x^2 & \mi 3x   & \mi 4    \\
		\hdashline
		\pl x^3  & \sd{\pl 2x^5} & \rd{\mi 3x^4} & \yd{\mi 4x^3} \\
		\hdashline
		\mi 2x^2 & \rd{\mi 4x^4} & \yd{\pl 6x^3} & \bd{\pl 8x^2} \\
		\hdashline
		\pl 2x   & \yd{\pl 4x^3} & \bd{\mi 6x^2} & \gd{\mi 8x}   \\
		\hdashline
		\pl 3    & \bd{\pl 6x^2} & \gd{\mi 9x}   & \sd{\mi 12}   \\
	\end{array}\\[1em]
	\parallel \\[1em]
		\sd{\pl 2x^5}
		\rd{\mi 3x^4}
		\rd{\mi 4x^4}
		\yd{\mi 4x^3}
		\yd{\pl 6x^3}
		\yd{\pl 4x^3}
		\bd{\pl 8x^2}
		\bd{\mi 6x^2}
		\bd{\pl 6x^2}
		\gd{\mi 8x}  
		\gd{\mi 9x}
		\sd{\mi 12}
	\\[1em]
	\parallel\\[1em]
	2x^5-7x^4+6x^3+8x^2-17x-12
$$

Notice that we included the signum for each term. Redundant as it may be, one of the most common mistakes in binomial multiplication is losing track of the signs. It's best to append every signum, then polish afterwards. Algebraic manipulation is similar to writing. Being concise requires trimming details, and if we trim the details too early, we increase the risk of error. On the topic of error, the "FOIL" method is the most common method taught in elementary schools, but in this author's experience, it is by far the most error-prone method. The method is essentially an arc diagram, which is _not_ an object humans are very good at processing. Accordingly, we won't address it here.


## Quadratic Functions
> __~definition: quadratic function~.__ Let ${q}$ be a fuction with ${\tx{dom}\px{q} \subseteq \reals}$ and ${\tx{codom}\px{q} \subseteq \reals.}$ Given ${x \in \tx{dom}\px{q},}$ if ${q}$ is of the form
> 
> $$
> 	q\px{x} = ax^2 + bx + c,
> $$
> 
> where ${a,}$ ${b,}$ and ${c}$ are constants with ${a \neq 0,}$ then we say that ${q}$ is a _quadratic function_. The term ${ax^2}$ is called the _quadratic term_, the term ${bx}$ is called the _linear term_, and the term ${c}$ is called the _constant term_. By definition, a quadratic function is a polynomial of degree 2.

## Derivatives of Polynomials

<Grid cols={2}>

> __~theorem~.__ Let ${p(x) = ax^b}$ be a polynomial function, where ${a}$ is a constant coefficient and ${b}$ is a constant exponent. Then
> 
> $$
> 	\di{}{x} \px{ax^b} = bax^{b-1}.
> $$

> __~theorem~.__ Let ${c}$ be a constant. Then
> 
> $$
> 	\di{}{x} c = 0.
> $$
