> __~definition~.__ Let ${p}$ be a function, where ${\tx{dom}{\px{p}} \subseteq \reals}$ and ${\tx{codom}\px{p} \subseteq \reals.}$ Then ${p}$ is a polynomial function if ${\ell}$ is of the form:
> 
> $$
> 	p(x) = a_{n}x^n + a_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \ldots + a_2x^2 + a_1x + a_0,
> $$
> 
> where each ${a}$ is a real number called a _coefficient_, and ${n}$ is a natural number corresponding to the _degree_ of the polynomial.

## Linear Functions
> __~definition~.__ Let ${\ell}$ be a function, where ${\tx{dom}\px{\ell} \subseteq \reals}$ and ${\tx{codom}\px{\ell} \subseteq \reals.}$  We say that ${\ell}$ is a _linear function_ if ${\ell}$ is of the form:
> 
> $$
> 	\ell{\px{x}} = ax + b,
> $$
> 
> where ${a}$ and ${b}$ are real number constants.

Linear functions are polynomial functions of degree 1. They are also the easiest functions to think about and grasp. So much so that their procedures — linear equations — have spawned an entire field of study, called [linear algebra](./math/linear_algebra/axioms).

## Horizontal and Vertical Lines
> __~horizontal line~.__ A horizontal line is defined as the linear function ${h(x) = a,}$ where ${a}$ is a constant.

> __~vertical line~.__ A vertical line is defined as the relation ${x = a,}$ where ${a}$ is a constant.

~remark~. The horizontal line ${h}$ is defined as a function, but the vertical line is not. Why? Because a vertical line is _not a function_. It maps a single element of its domain to all of the elements of its codomain, and this does not satisfy the definition of a function.

### Monotonicity of Linear Functions
> __~monotonicity~.__ Let ${\ell\px{x}=ax + b}$ be a linear function, with ${\tx{dom}\px{\ell} \subseteq \reals}$ and ${\tx{codom}\px{\ell} \subseteq \reals.}$
> - If ${a \lt 0,}$ then ${\ell}$ is _decreasing_.
> - If ${a = 0,}$ then ${\ell}$ is _constant_.
> - If ${a \gt 0,}$ then ${\ell}$ is _increasing_.

### ${\bm{x}}$-intercept
> __definition.__ Let ${\ell\px{x}=ax ~\pl~ b}$ be a linear function, with ${\tx{dom}\px{\ell} \subseteq \reals}$ and ${\tx{codom}\px{\ell} \subseteq \reals.}$ The ${x}$-intercept of ${\ell}$ is the set ${\set{x \in \tx{dom}{\px{f}} : \ell{\px{x}}=0}.}$

~example~. Let ${f(x)=2x ~\pl~ 1.}$ The ${x}$-intercept is ${\set{\mi\frac{1}{2}},}$ since:

$$
\eqs{
	0 &= 2x ~\pl~ 1 \\
	0 ~\mi~ 1 &= 2x ~\pl~ 1 ~\mi~ 1 \\
	\mi1 &= 2x ~\pl~ 0 \\
	\mi1 &= 2x \\[0.5em]
	\frac{\mi1}{2} &= \frac{2x}{2} \\[0.5em]
	\mi\frac{1}{2} &= x
}
$$

### Derivatives of Linear Functions
> __~derivative: linear function~.__ Let ${\ell\px{x}=ax + b}$ be a linear function, with ${\tx{dom}\px{\ell} \subseteq \reals}$ and ${\tx{codom}\px{\ell} \subseteq \reals.}$ Then ${a}$ corresponds to the derivative of ${\ell.}$ Where ${x_1, x_2 \in \tx{dom}\px{\ell}, x_1 \le x_2,}$ ${a}$ has the property:
> 
> $$
> 	a = \frac{f(x_2) - f(x_1)}{x_2 - x_1}.
> $$

<Algo>

__~linslope~.__ Let ${a=\ix{x_1, y_1}}$ and ${b=\ix{x_2, y_2}}$ be arrays of length 2, where ${a,b \in \df{number} \times \df{number}.}$ The procedure ${\df{linslope}}$ returns the derivative of the line passing through ${a}$ and ${b.}$

1. __function__ ${\df{linslope}(a,b)}$
	1. __if__ ${\len\px{a}=2 \land \len\px{b}=2}$ __then__
		1. ${\let{\Delta y}{a\ix{1}-b\ix{1}}}$
		2. ${\let{\Delta x}{a\ix{0}-b\ix{0}}}$
		3. __return__ ${\dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}}$

</Algo>

### Parallel and Parpendicular Lines
> __~relation~.__ Let ${f(x) = a_fx ~\pl~ b_f}$ and ${g(x) = a_g x ~\pl~ b_g}$ be linear functions. If, and only if, ${a_f = b_f,}$ then ${f}$ and ${g}$ are _parallel_, and we write ${f \parallel g.}$ If, and only if, ${a_f \by a_g = -1,}$ then ${f}$ and ${g}$ are _perpendicular_ and we write ${f \perp g.}$
